Artificial Intelligence

Artificial intelligence originated in the 1950s, driven by pioneers like Alan Turing and the foundational 1956 Dartmouth Conference, which proposed that machines could simulate human intelligence through logical reasoning and symbolic processing. While early "AI winters" slowed progress due to limited computing power, the field saw a massive revival in the 2010s with the rise of machine learning, big data, and neural networks, leading to breakthroughs in image and speech recognition. Currently, the field has shifted from rule-based programming to generative AI, enabling systems to create, reason, and operate autonomously. Expected progression in 2026 and beyond involves the development of "agentic AI," where systems plan, act independently, and integrate directly into scientific discovery and complex decision-making processes, rather than just providing assistive content. The long-term trajectory focuses on achieving higher levels of autonomy and personalization, ultimately aiming toward Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) while managing growing demands for ethical and responsible AI development.
